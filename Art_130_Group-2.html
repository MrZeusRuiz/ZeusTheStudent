Donovan Quintero | Photographer
Artis bio by Zeus



There is beauty throughout this land of ours we call America. In the Southwest, you might find red-stained cliffs that contrast the bluest of skies while the white fluffy clouds split the frame to lure your eyes to the single lonely coyote atop a sand dune. That’s what Photographer Donovan Quintero is out looking for.

Mr. Quintero is the man out in search of hidden treasure not likely seen beyond those who grew up locally. There are stories to tell through the lens and Mr Quintero is out exploring the land in search of them. The stories he finds often include people. His camera captures each wrinkle and imperfection on an elderly woman's cheek with such high definition that you can surely read the life she’s had. Instantly there are thoughts of lineage, motherhood, work, and love—her story has been captured and added to a catalog of other stories like her own. These photographs mean something. These are the photographs of the indigenous people of this land.

Significance

Mr. Quintero’s work during the Covid pandemic can now be added to the aforementioned captured stories. During the time when the entire world became reclusive whilst hoarding toilet paper, Mr. Donovan’s lens was shining a spotlight on the marginalized communities of Indigenous people. Again, the Native identity was left behind in a time of need while the rest of America hashtagged the phrase, “We’re all in this together”.

Camille Seaman | Photographer
Artist bio by Zeus


Camille Seaman is an American photographer known for her stunning photographs of natural landscapes, particularly icebergs and storms. She was born on July 22, 1969, in Middletown, Connecticut, United States. Seaman has a deep connection with nature and has been passionate about photography since her childhood. She has a unique ability to capture the beauty and power of natural phenomena, often focusing on the relationship between humans and their environment. Her work explores themes of climate change, environmental awareness, and the fragility of our planet.

Significance

Without deviating from her passions, Ms Seaman explores identity through her lens. The sharp contrast between technology and traditional headwear is obvious—but look closer, there is a commentary to dive into. What does it mean to be “native” today in modern America? And who gets to decide?

Darren Solomon | Artist
Artist Bio by Francisco Ruvalcaba

Darren Solomon is a Brooklyn composer who began his music career at the age of 19. He attained a music degree from NYU and went on tour with Barry Manilow for four years, where he began composing. He has done music for over 500 commercials, including commercials for Pepsi, L’Oreal, MnM’s, and more. Solomon has always loved the intersection between art and technology. Although he is a music-based person, this has not stopped him from dabbling in technology-based projects that push forth a combination of the two. His most well-known project is In Bb 2.0, which is a crowd-sourced project that broke new ground.

Solomon is enjoying his multifaceted career in music, including performing, exploring applications for music in new media, composing for dance companies, and curating and producing live shows.

In Bb 2.0
In Bb 2.0 (pronounced B-flat) Solomon takes Identity and Place and makes an example of it. In Bb 2.0 is an art project that is interactive, allowing the user to play and start various videos, which were crowd-sourced, to play together. No matter how one goes about playing these videos, they will always fit together, always sound in tandem, and can be looped in any way the user chooses. This art relates to Identity and Place by doing away with them. It gets rid of singular identity and the need to be nearby. All individuals, from different backgrounds and different homes, come together and create a musical piece in an awe-inspiring fashion. This piece tells the observer that no matter who you are, what your identity is, where you were born, where you came from, or what "place" you call home, you can come together with others who are different from you and create.


maker/music

maker/music is a bit of an inversion of Solomon’s first piece of In Bb 2.0. Instead of being crowd sourced by the greater internet it was sourced via the students of Northern State University and community members of Aberdeen. Unlike the first project Solomon wanted to see what could be done by those in a close proximity and what they as individuals in the same place could end up creating. With over 70 submissions they narrowed down 12 and created something a-kin to In Bb 2.0. maker/music relates to identity and place by bringing a scope of a location, taking those who live in a certain area and making from that location. It relates to identity by having those who are self identified community members come forth to participate, being what it is that they can, and what they feel can fit. Each one having different tools they bring, knowledge of a different language, music, one is simply a person walking. All unique, all different, all individuals.































































Anselm Kiefer | Artist
Artist bio by Francisco Ruvalcaba

Anselm Kiefer is a German artist who primarily paints and sculpts. He was born in 1945 and features a lot of work that touches on the history and taboos of German culture. His work incorporates different elements such as straw, lead, ash, clay, and shellac. His works are characterized by their unflinching willingness to talk about the dark blemishes of German history. Kiefer holds his materials to value and holds a "spiritual connection" and aims to draw out the life that he feels is in his raw materials, he draws it out by transforming physically or letting it set in acid baths. In 1990 Kiefer was awarded the Wolf Award, in 1999 he was awarded the Praemium Imperiale by the Japanese Art Association for his life's works and in 2008 he was awarded the Peace Prize of the German Book Trade, which he was the first-ever visual artist to ever receive.

March Heath

Anselm Kiefer's March Heath is a painting featuring a German landscape right after the end of World War II. It depicts a field that is reminiscent of decay and death and speaks to the viewer about the loss of what once was. This piece relates to Identity and Place by Kiefer being a German who confronts a dark aspect of history with the Holocaust and putting it into a visible place. To be able to know that your people have this history that is looked down upon and that you have to carry this burden, to speak of it and make it known and to learn from it. It's what makes it part of an identity, one he does not shy away from, one he holds to regard and transforms that aspect visually, an ugly truth. A landscape death, from inner turmoil. A display of self to the world and how they can be in tandem with each other.

The Red Sea

Kiefer's Red Sea is one of his most well-renowned paintings. It's a symbolic work of a hard and dark landscape with a seemingly sea-like appearance, with a boat filled with red in it. Above it, abstraction and the difficulty of making out what this painting's reality is. This painting relates to identity and place by some of the same means as the previous but with a different flavor. Here it looks almost as if the sea were to be a merging place with a room, perhaps alluding to horrors that have taken place. It tackles identity with the grasp of knowing what has been committed and not turning away. Taking a stand to its horror and acknowledging it for what it is, history.
